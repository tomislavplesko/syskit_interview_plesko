

We analysed six months of product activity, billing data, and CRM records across approximately 500 customer accounts. Here is the honest picture:

- **Roughly one in four accounts is either at risk or has already churned.** Of the active base, a meaningful segment has declining product engagement with little to no recent contact from Customer Success.
- **The team has been flying partly blind.** A significant number of accounts that churned had no Customer Success contact in their final two months. These customers did not ask for help — they simply left. The warning signs were visible in the product data weeks before they cancelled.
- **There is real growth sitting untapped.** A set of existing customers is actively expanding their use of the Power Platform integration and engaging multiple users. These accounts are on starter or business plans and show the profile of customers ready to move up.

---

## The Three Findings That Matter Most

### 1. Silent churn is the biggest avoidable revenue leak

A subset of churned accounts had zero Customer Success contact in the 60 days before they left. Their product usage had been declining for weeks — visible in our data — but no one reached out. This is not a relationship problem; it is a visibility problem. The data existed. The alert system did not.

**What this means:** We are losing accounts that could be saved. The question is not whether we can predict churn — we can. The question is whether we act on those predictions before the renewal conversation is too late.

### 2. Referral and partner channels bring the customers who stay

When we compare acquisition channels not just by volume but by the quality of customers they produce, two channels stand out: referral and partner. These accounts have lower churn rates, higher average contract values, and a higher proportion of healthy, deeply engaged tenants. By contrast, some paid channels bring in customers at high volume with materially higher churn rates — meaning we are investing budget to acquire customers who leave.

**What this means:** The marketing budget allocation does not reflect where value actually comes from. Shifting even a modest portion of spend toward referral incentives and partner development would likely improve net revenue retention.

### 3. The health score gives us early warning we did not have before

We built a scoring model that rates every customer from 0 to 100 based on how broadly they use the product, whether usage is growing or declining, how recently Customer Success has been in contact, and whether users are performing high-value actions. This score is predictive of churn — accounts that score below 40 are at substantially higher risk.

**What this means:** For the first time, Customer Success can prioritise their week based on objective signals rather than gut feel or last call dates. The score updates with the data. The dashboard is live and filterable by CSM, region, and plan.

### 4. The biggest funnel loss is after trial engagement, not at acquisition

Using trial-sourced accounts as a cohort, we built a transparent trial-to-paid proxy funnel (trial -> POV started -> paying -> retained -> healthy retained). The largest drop happens after initial trial engagement, indicating the main gap is conversion quality and onboarding execution rather than top-of-funnel lead volume.

**What this means:** We should spend less time asking for more trial traffic and more time improving the handoff from trial to successful paid adoption.

## Recommended Actions

### Next Week
- **Review every account scoring below 40** that has a renewal in the next 90 days. These are the highest-priority calls. The at-risk renewal list is available on the dashboard's "At-Risk & Renewals" page, sorted by urgency.
- **Brief the CS team on the blind spot accounts.** Pull up the list of accounts that churned with no recent contact. Run a short retrospective: at what point in the usage data could we have intervened?

### Next Month
- **Activate the health score as a CSM workflow tool.** Set a rule: any account that drops below 60 and has not been contacted in 30 days triggers an automatic CSM task. This does not require new tooling — just a process decision.
- **Run a targeted outreach campaign on expansion candidates.** Our analysis identified a group of business-plan customers whose Power Platform usage is growing and who have multiple active users. These accounts are warm. A focused conversation about what they are trying to do next is likely to convert to upsells.
- **Bring the marketing channel findings to the next budget review.** Share the churn-rate-by-channel data with Marketing. The conversation is not about blame — it is about rebalancing acquisition spend toward channels that produce customers who grow.
- **Install a trial-conversion handoff playbook.** Require an onboarding checklist for every trial that starts POV, and trigger CS outreach if usage does not grow in the first two weeks.

### Next Quarter
- **Build an early warning alert system.** The health score should trigger proactive outreach automatically — not require someone to check a dashboard. A weekly Slack digest sent to each CSM showing their accounts that declined more than 10 points in the last four weeks would cost almost nothing to build and would close the blind spot gap permanently.
- **Invest in referral programme infrastructure.** If referral customers retain better and spend more, the ROI on a formal referral incentive programme is likely to be high. Run a small test, measure results against the same channels currently receiving larger budgets.
- **Re-evaluate trial-to-paid conversion.** A meaningful portion of the pipeline consists of trial accounts that never converted. A focused analysis of where trial accounts drop off — which features they did or did not use — would give the Sales and Product teams a clear playbook for improving that conversion rate.

## One Prediction That Would Change How CS Spends Its Time

If we could predict one thing: **which account will go quiet in the next 30 days without telling us**.

Right now, CS time is allocated based on renewal proximity and relationship familiarity. The accounts that need attention most are often not the ones that ask for it. A model that identifies — four to six weeks in advance — which accounts are about to stop logging in, stop running reports, and stop responding to emails would let CS redirect effort to exactly those accounts before they reach the point of no return.

We have built the first version of this model. It is in the dashboard under "Churn Prediction." It is a starting point, not a finished product. With real operational data and six more months of history, the accuracy will improve. The framework is in place.
